# Failure patterns

Nine real Apple/Jobs-era failures, distilled to the pattern, the trigger phrase that should fire in your head when you're about to repeat it, and the translation to software/content/personal-project work. Read these when refining — most refinement is recognizing the failure mode early.

## 1. Lisa — tech-led pricing

**The pattern.** A product designed around what the technology can do, priced for the audience the team imagines, shipped to a market that won't pay it.

**What happened.** The Lisa shipped at $9,995 in 1983 — Fortune 500 pricing for a personal computer. Apple, founded on selling to people, had drifted into corporate-IT thinking. The Mac shipped two years later at a quarter of the price and ate Lisa's lunch from the same building.

**Trigger phrase.** "We need this to cover the engineering cost." If the price has to be high to justify the build, the build was wrong.

**In software/content terms.** Subscription tiers reverse-engineered to recover investor money. Features bundled to justify the spec list. Premium tiers nobody asks for. The customer was never the target; the funding model was.

## 2. NeXT cube — aesthetics over substance

**The pattern.** A beautiful artifact, perfectly engineered, with no software ecosystem and no use-case match. Shipping the object you wanted to make instead of the product the world wanted.

**What happened.** Magnesium cube, magneto-optical drive, NeXTSTEP. Stunning. Tim Berners-Lee built the first web browser on one. About 50,000 units shipped over five years before NeXT exited hardware. Beautiful failure.

**Trigger phrase.** "But look at it — it's incredible." Aesthetic conviction without distribution, ecosystem, or audience.

**In software/content terms.** A well-designed app with no growth loop. An SDK with elegant API and no reason to switch. Open-source projects whose README is gorgeous and whose first issue is from 2021.

## 3. Apple Cube — your taste, not the market's

**The pattern.** Idiosyncratic taste imposed without checking demand. The operator builds for themselves; the market is smaller than imagined.

**What happened.** The Power Mac G4 Cube (2000) — gorgeous, transparent acrylic, $1,799 with no monitor. About 150,000 units in twelve months before suspension. Jobs admitted later: "I wanted the flat-panel display but I don't need the features of the Power Mac." Customer experience filtered through one customer: him.

**Trigger phrase.** "Anyone who appreciates design will see it." When the audience is "anyone with taste like mine," the audience is one.

**In software/content terms.** Personal-project shipping disguised as a product. Tools you'd want that nobody else wants. The "I'd use this" market is yourself plus zero confidants. The fix is usually not better marketing; it's a real audience or a different product.

## 4. MobileMe — public humiliation as accountability

**The pattern.** Using public berating as a forcing function. Sometimes resets a team; usually drives out the talent that had options.

**What happened.** July 2008 — MobileMe shipped broken: lost emails, sync errors, a withering Mossberg review. Jobs assembled the team in Town Hall: "Can anyone tell me what MobileMe is supposed to do? So why the fuck doesn't it do that?" Eddy Cue was named owner on the spot. The eventual iCloud was real; the cost in talent flight was also real.

**Trigger phrase.** "We need to make this person an example." Almost never necessary. Pick the failure mode you can absorb.

**In software/content terms.** Twitter pile-ons of the engineer who shipped the bug. Public Slack callouts. Shame-driven retros. The accountability is real; the method is replaceable. Written DRI assignments and structured post-mortems get the same outcome without the cost.

## 5. Antennagate — defending the design against experiential failure

**The pattern.** When customer experience contradicts a design choice, the design choice was wrong. Defending it doubles the loss.

**What happened.** iPhone 4 (2010) dropped calls when held in the natural left-hand position because skin contact bridged two antenna sections. Jobs's first email to a customer: "Just avoid holding it in this way." The press summarized it as "you're holding it wrong." Eventually a press conference, free bumpers, settlement. The design was a constraint; the experience was a failure mode that should have killed the design.

**Trigger phrase.** "Users are using it wrong." If the natural use is the failure case, the design owns the failure.

**In software/content terms.** "That's not how the feature is supposed to work." The user's natural path produces the bug; the team defends the spec. The argument is lost the moment it begins.

## 6. App Store reversal — the wrong "no," reversed fast

**This one is what to do, not what to avoid.** It is the principle, not the failure.

**What happened.** WWDC June 2007 — Jobs told developers Apple had "a very sweet solution": build web apps for iPhone using Safari and HTML5. Developer reaction was scathing. October 17, 2007 — four months later — Jobs published an open letter: "Let me just say it: We want native third party applications on the iPhone, and we plan to have an SDK in developers' hands in February." The App Store launched July 10, 2008.

**The takeaway.** A "no" reversed in four months is part of the system. A "no" defended for years that should have been "yes" is the failure. When the call was wrong and the new path is operationally feasible, reverse fast — the speed of the reversal is the credibility, not the original call.

**Trigger phrase to recognize when this applies.** "We already announced X." The announcement isn't a contract — the product is. Most "we said we wouldn't" debates would be over in 30 seconds if the original call had been a phone call instead of a tweet.

**In software/content terms.** Roadmap items defended past their sell-by date. Product positioning held because reversing would "look bad." The reversal almost always reads as confidence — if it ships within months and the new path is real.

## 7. Stock options backdating — "the rules are different for us"

**The pattern.** A culture that thinks of itself as exempt from external rules — securities law, accounting standards, content policy, regulatory reality. The integrated-stack philosophy does not extend to integrated governance.

**What happened.** 2006 — Apple's internal probe found stock option backdating between 1997 and 2001. Two executives resigned; one was charged by the SEC. Jobs personally was not found culpable, but the cultural posture — "we're different from regular companies" — was the actual problem.

**Trigger phrase.** "Other companies have to do that, but we..." If the sentence ends "we don't, because of how we work," it is usually wrong.

**In software/content terms.** Privacy policies that don't match implementation. Terms of service the product clearly violates. "We're a startup, GDPR/SOC2/COPPA doesn't apply yet." It does. The exemption you imagine isn't there.

## 8. Cancer-treatment delay — conviction outside your domain

**The pattern.** Trusting your own conviction over external expertise in a domain where you have no calibrated judgment. In domains you've earned mastery, conviction is correct. In domains you haven't, conviction is a category error.

**What happened.** Jobs delayed surgery for nine months in 2003-2004 in favor of alternative regimens — vegan-juice diets, acupuncture, herbal remedies, bowel cleanses — for a treatable form of cancer. He told Isaacson later, with regret: "I really didn't want them to open up my body, so I tried to see if a few other things would work." The same conviction-driven decision-making that produced the iPhone produced a nine-month hesitation on a treatable disease.

**Trigger phrase.** "I have a feeling about this one." In a domain where you've shipped a thousand decisions, the feeling is signal. In a domain where you've shipped zero, the feeling is just feelings.

**In software/content terms.** Designers making security calls. Engineers making legal calls. Founders making financial-controls calls. Each domain has experts whose career-long calibration is the data; the founder's conviction is the noise.

## 9. Personal-cost pattern — the system's tax

**The pattern.** A system that runs on the operator's conviction without empathy at the personal scale produces great products *and* concentrated personal harm. The harm is not a feature of the system. It is a tax it imposed on people who didn't sign up to pay it.

**What happened.** Jobs denied paternity of his daughter Lisa in 1979 even after a DNA test (94.41% probability). His treatment of subordinates during the Mac years was harsh and frequently humiliating. Hertzfeld documents that Jobs once told him in passing he was easily replaceable. The later Jobs (post-2005) achieved most of the same outcomes with markedly less violence.

**Trigger phrase.** "It's just business." If the cost is being paid by people who can't push back — junior people, contractors, family, users you'll never meet — it's not "just business." It is the tax, made visible.

**In software/content terms.** Burning out a team to hit a launch. Treating customers as inputs. Crunch as default. The work can be done at the same standard with proportional accountability instead of personal violence; the choice between them is cultural, not operational.

## How to use these

When refining, ask: which of these is this about to become? Most of the time the answer is one of three:

- **Apple Cube** (your taste, not the market's)
- **Antennagate** (defending the design against experiential failure)
- **App Store reversal** (the call was wrong; reverse fast — this is the move, not the trap)

The other six are rarer but the recognition matters more — they're the patterns that destroy companies, not just products. If you find yourself in #4 (MobileMe), #7 (stock options), #8 (cancer), or #9 (personal cost), the refinement isn't about the artifact — it's about what the operator is willing to pay and who's paying it.
