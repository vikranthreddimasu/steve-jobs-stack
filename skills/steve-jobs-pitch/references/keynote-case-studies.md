# Keynote case studies

Six Jobs keynote introductions, distilled to the load-bearing moves. Read these when shaping a launch, demo, talk, or any narrative artifact that needs more than a single line. Each entry names what worked and the technique to steal.

## 1984 Macintosh — January 24, 1984, Flint Center

**The setup.** Personal computing was IBM and CP/M — utilitarian, beige, alien to most people. Apple had the Apple II but no claim on the future of computing.

**The arc.** Jobs opened by reading from Bob Dylan's "The Times They Are A-Changin'" — placing the world-before frame culturally, not technically. He ran the Ridley Scott "1984" Super Bowl commercial — the antagonist, named theatrically. Then he unboxed the Macintosh and let it speak. A synthesized voice introduction: "Hello, I am Macintosh... Unaccustomed as I am to public speaking, I'd like to share with you a maxim I thought of the first time I met an IBM mainframe: never trust a computer you can't lift." The product introduced itself.

**The anchor.** The Mac speaking. Jobs got out of the way and let the device be the demo.

**The technique.** The most powerful demo is one where the product makes its own case and the operator stops talking. If your product can do the work in 30 seconds without commentary, commentary is in the way.

## iMac — May 6, 1998, Flint Center

**The setup.** Apple had just survived bankruptcy. The PC industry was beige boxes for office work. The internet was widespread but consumer setup was a multi-step ordeal.

**The arc.** Jobs framed iMac as Apple's consumer return. He named the antagonist — "step one, step two, step three" of the typical PC internet setup, a ritual everyone had endured. Then: "Step one: plug in. Step two: get connected. Step three — there is no step three." The Bondi Blue translucent shell appeared. "The 'i' stands for internet. Also for individual, instruct, inform, inspire."

**The anchor.** "There is no step three." A tagline that is also a feature description that is also a punchline.

**The technique.** The strongest taglines aren't slogans pasted on; they are compressed product descriptions you remember because they describe the actual experience. If your tagline doesn't pass for a one-line demo summary, it's marketing.

## iPod — October 23, 2001, Town Hall, Apple HQ

**The setup.** MP3 players existed and were either bulky-with-storage or small-with-tiny-storage. Music was being pirated through Napster; the labels were panicking. Hardware specs were quoted in megabytes.

**The arc.** Jobs walked through the deficiencies of competing players, set the iPod's spec — 5GB hard drive, FireWire — then translated the spec into experience: "1,000 songs in your pocket." Not megabytes. Songs. Then: "I happen to have one right here in my pocket," and pulled it out. Within minutes, apple.com's homepage carried "1,000 songs in your pocket" as the headline.

**The anchor.** "1,000 songs in your pocket" — and the literal pocket gesture.

**The technique.** Translate every spec into a unit the audience already cares about. Megabytes are an engineering measurement; songs are a memory of mixtapes. If you're stating numbers in your own internal units, you haven't finished the work.

## iPhone — January 9, 2007, Macworld, San Francisco

**The setup.** Smartphones were keypads with browsers on top — BlackBerry, Treo, Nokia E-series. The category was business. Touchscreens existed in industrial use; nobody had one in a phone.

**The arc.** Jobs opened with stillness: "This is a day I've been looking forward to for two and a half years." He then said: "Today we're introducing three revolutionary products of this class. The first one is a wide-screen iPod with touch controls. The second is a revolutionary mobile phone. And the third is a breakthrough internet communications device." He repeated the three. Then: "Are you getting it? These are not three separate devices. This is one device. And we are calling it iPhone." The audience had to do the math; when they got it, it was theirs.

**The anchor.** Three products that turned out to be one. The audience participating in the realization is what made the moment.

**The technique.** If you can structure the reveal so the audience completes the last step themselves, they remember it as their own discovery rather than your announcement. The setup does more work than the reveal.

## MacBook Air — January 15, 2008, Macworld

**The setup.** Laptops were getting more capable but heavier. Subnotebooks (Sony TZ series) compromised on screen, keyboard, or performance. Apple had no thin-and-light story.

**The arc.** Jobs walked through the constraints any thin notebook had to keep — full-size keyboard, full 13-inch display, full performance — and rejected the compromises others had made. Then: "It's so thin, it even fits inside one of these envelopes that we've all seen floating around the office." He reached behind the lectern, pulled out a manila envelope, slid the MacBook Air out of it.

**The anchor.** The manila envelope. A physical gesture so specific it became the product's identity. Tim Cook still cites it more than fifteen years later.

**The technique.** Find the single physical or visible move that *is* the positioning. Not a metaphor for the positioning — the actual thing, in the actual moment, in front of people. The envelope didn't symbolize thin; it proved thin.

## iPad — January 27, 2010, Yerba Buena Center for the Arts

**The setup.** Tablets had failed for a decade. Microsoft had pushed Tablet PCs as laptops-with-stylus; they did not catch on. Netbooks were the cheap-laptop story everyone was telling.

**The arc.** Jobs framed the question — "Is there room for a third category of device, between a laptop and a smartphone?" — and answered it conditionally: only if it was demonstrably better at certain things than either. He attacked netbooks directly: "They aren't better at anything." Then he sat down in a Le Corbusier LC-3 chair and used the iPad. The chair-and-iPad tableau communicated the device's positioning more economically than any spec.

**The anchor.** The chair. It said: this is a thing you use sitting on a couch, not at a desk. It changed the category by changing the body posture.

**The technique.** Sometimes positioning lives in the body language around the product, not the product itself. Show how it's held, where it's used, who's using it. The chair told you the use case before the demo started.

## What to take from the six

- **The setup matters more than the reveal.** Five of six keynotes spend most of their oxygen on the world-before and the antagonist. The reveal is brief because the setup carried the weight.
- **The anchor is one specific thing, not a list.** A pocket. An envelope. A chair. A line. Pick one.
- **The audience doing the last step beats explaining it.** "Are you getting it?" beats "let me explain." Trust the room.
- **Spec → experience translation is non-negotiable.** No keynote here states the metric in engineering units. Every number becomes something the audience already cares about.
- **The anchor outlives the keynote.** "1,000 songs in your pocket," "step three doesn't exist," the manila envelope, the chair — these are what the press carried, what the headlines used, what people remembered a decade later. Pick the anchor first; the keynote serves it.
