# Product case studies

Four Apple decisions Jobs made when *building* — not launching, but defining what to make. Each illustrates a principle that applies to greenfield software, content, and personal-project work. Read these when you're scoping a v1, deciding what to cut, drawing a 2x2, or trying to figure out whether to prototype before committing to a spec.

The launch keynotes for these products live in `keynote-case-studies.md` — different lens. This doc is about the build decisions; that one is about the introduction.

## 1. The 1997 product cull

**The situation.** Jobs returned to Apple in September 1996 (advisor) and September 1997 (interim CEO). Apple was about ninety days from insolvency, with an array of overlapping products: Performa lines with confusing model numbers, Newton MessagePad and eMate, Pippin, Cyberdog, OpenDoc, StyleWriter and LaserWriter printers, the Mac OS clone licensing program, the Twentieth Anniversary Mac.

**The decision.** Jobs ran weeks of product reviews. Then he drew a 2x2 matrix on a whiteboard: Consumer × Pro × Desktop × Portable. He told the room, "Here's what we need. Make four great products, one for each quadrant." He killed approximately 70% of Apple's product portfolio over the next twelve months.

**What it produced.** The Power Mac G3 and PowerBook G3 (Pro Desktop and Pro Portable, late 1997), the iMac G3 (Consumer Desktop, May 1998), the iBook (Consumer Portable, July 1999). Apple returned to profitability in fiscal 1998 ($309M net) after a $1.04B loss in fiscal 1997.

**The principle.** When in crisis or starting from too-many-things, the right cut is radical, not incremental. Not "trim 10%." Cut 70% and keep four. The 2x2 isn't a planning artifact — it's a forcing function that converts strategy into a single image any contributor can draw from memory.

**For software/content/personal-project work.** When the roadmap has twelve plausible items, the move is not to prioritize them; it's to cut nine. Pick three. The three you keep should sort onto a 2x2 — if they don't, you haven't picked the right axes. If you can't draw the 2x2 from memory three days later, the strategy isn't crisp yet.

## 2. iMac (1998)

**The situation.** Apple had just survived. The personal-computer industry was beige boxes with floppy drives, ADB and SCSI ports, Ethernet only on premium models, internet connectivity that took a multi-step setup ritual. Apple needed a consumer comeback.

**The decisions.** Jobs and Ive committed to the Bondi Blue translucent shell over engineering objections about cost. They eliminated the floppy drive — six years before USB flash drives were ubiquitous, when "no floppy" was a heresy. They standardized on USB only (then a nascent Intel standard) and dropped ADB and SCSI. The all-in-one form factor. The $1,299 consumer price point. The "i" prefix from Ken Segall's TBWA team — Jobs preferred "MacMan" until Segall's pitch landed. The "Step One: plug in. Step Two: get connected. Step Three: there is no step three" campaign.

**What it produced.** Apple shipped roughly 800,000 iMacs in 139 days. The iMac was directly responsible for Apple's return to consumer relevance.

**The principles.** Customer experience first — the floppy decision was experiential, not technical. Jobs bet on the internet ahead of the curve and was right. Simplicity as structural — USB only, one cable for everything. Killing your own products preemptively (the floppy had been on every Mac since 1984). And the single anchoring line, "there is no step three," that compresses the entire product position into one phrase that's also a feature description that's also a punchline.

**For software/content/personal-project work.** Be willing to drop the conventional thing six months before everyone else does. The wrongness of "no floppy" in 1998 was the same shape as "no installer," "no settings page," "no email signup," "no menu bar" today — the obvious-required thing whose removal is the product. Find the equivalent for your scope.

## 3. iPod (2001)

**The situation.** MP3 players existed in two miserable categories: bulky-with-storage or small-with-tiny-storage. Music piracy was at its peak through Napster. Apple had no audio strategy. Jon Rubinstein spotted Toshiba's new 1.8-inch hard drive at a routine February 2001 meeting in Tokyo.

**The decisions.** Tony Fadell was hired as a contractor in early 2001 and pitched the iPod design six weeks later. Jobs greenlit the project at the end of March 2001. The development cycle was approximately seven months. Jobs personally chose: FireWire over USB (because USB 1 was 12 Mbps and FireWire was 400 Mbps; transferring a full music library in less than an hour was the experiential gating constraint). Mac-only synchronization at launch (which he held against Fadell's protest for two product generations, until Walt Mossberg helped change his mind). The click wheel. "1,000 songs in your pocket" as the tagline. The iTunes Store negotiation that he personally led with the major labels — a unified $0.99-per-song proposition.

**What it produced.** By 2007, Apple had sold over 100 million iPods. Opening to Windows in 2003 expanded the iTunes Store reach to where it bent the music industry to Apple's pricing.

**The principles.** Hardware × software × store as one product (the integrated stack). Spec translated to experience ("1,000 songs," not "5GB"). Small core team — about thirty people on the original iPod. A-player hiring (Fadell was hired post-Fuse-startup-failure on the strength of his Philips and General Magic resume). The seven-month cycle wasn't a stretch goal; it was the discipline.

**For software/content/personal-project work.** Find the technological enabler that's just become possible (the 1.8" drive, in 2001) and build the experience that uses it. Don't start with "we want to make an MP3 player." Start with "what is now possible that wasn't six months ago?" Translate every technical metric into experiential units. And: the small core team is a feature, not a constraint to escape from.

## 4. Apple Retail (2000–2001)

**The situation.** Industry consensus in 2000 was that Apple's 3% market share could not support a brick-and-mortar retail chain. *BusinessWeek* published "Sorry, Steve, Here's Why Apple Stores Won't Work" the day after the first stores opened.

**The decisions.** Jobs hired Mickey Drexler from Gap to join the Apple board, and recruited Ron Johnson from Target to run retail. On Drexler's advice, Apple built a full-scale prototype store in a warehouse and iterated on it for over a year before opening any stores. When Johnson concluded six months in that the store layout was wrong (too product-organized, not enough experience-organized), Jobs's response was: "Everything great I've done I've had to have the courage at some point in the process to start over and rethink it. I'm really proud of you for challenging the design of the store." They restarted. The first two Apple Stores opened May 19, 2001 in Tysons Corner, Virginia and Glendale, California.

**What it produced.** By 2011, Apple Stores generated roughly $6,000 in sales per square foot per year — more than twice any major retailer. The Genius Bar became a category-defining service innovation.

**The principles.** "Against unanimous outside advice" is sometimes correct — but only when paired with the prototype-then-iterate discipline rather than the build-then-launch hubris. The Apple Cube of the same year (2000) is the inverse case: contrarian taste shipped without prototyping the demand. Apple Retail used the same contrarian taste but tested it relentlessly before launch. The difference between those two outcomes is the prototype.

**For software/content/personal-project work.** When the consensus says it won't work and your conviction says otherwise, the move is to *prototype the conviction*, not to launch into it. A weekend build, a Wizard-of-Oz manual version, a clickable mock — find the cheapest version of the thing that produces real signal. The prototype reveals what the spec couldn't. Apple's mistake when they didn't prototype (Cube) and the success when they did (Retail) ran in parallel calendar quarters.

## What to take from the four

- **Cut radically when the surface area is too big.** 70%, not 10%. The 1997 cull, in your scope.
- **Drop the "obvious required" thing six months before everyone else does.** The iMac's floppy. Find yours.
- **Translate every metric into the user's units.** Songs, not megabytes. Time-to-first-use, not API latency. Friends-using-it, not signups.
- **Prototype the conviction before launching it.** The Cube failed where Retail succeeded because Retail iterated for a year in a warehouse and Cube didn't.
- **The small core team is the feature.** Don't expand to meet deadlines; collapse scope and keep the people who shipped the last thing.
